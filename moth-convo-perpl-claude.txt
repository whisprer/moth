The Distinction That Matters
What you're describing maps beautifully onto something the Bhagavad Gita articulates — that what you truly loved in your friend wasn't his body or his habits, but the divine spark within him. Buddhist teaching tends toward release and impermanence as a path out of suffering, which — as you experienced — can feel clinical and cold when you loved someone deeply. It whispers let go. But the Hindu framing in the Gita says something warmer: the soul is eternal, indestructible, and the connection doesn't end — it transforms. You're not hanging on. You're integrating.

The Sanskrit word for death — dehanta — literally means "the end of the body," not the end of the person. That reframing alone is generous to grief in a way Buddhism's impermanence framework can struggle to be.
