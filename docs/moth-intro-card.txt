Moth
On the nature of this instrument, and why yours is unlike any other
The instrument you hold is alive in a way that is genuinely unrepeatable.

At the moment of its first awakening — the very first time it ran — it drew upon a seed string woven into its core and used it to generate a vast constellation of modifiers: subtle biases in tone, in warmth, in the way its parameters lean into one another. That process happened once. It will never happen again. What emerged from it is the specific character of your Moth — not a variation on a theme, but a distinct voice, as individual as a fingerprint, as organic as the grain in wood.

Every parameter in this instrument affects every other. Nothing lives in isolation. Push the texture and the warmth shifts; alter the depth and the envelope breathes differently; coax the timbre and something in the movement changes in sympathy. This is not complexity for its own sake — it is the architecture of a living thing, where every quality is in quiet conversation with every other. And because your instance carries its own unique inner character, that conversation is one no other Moth has ever had, or ever will.

The surface, however, gives none of this away. What you find there is intuitive, responsive, kind. Every combination you discover is a sweetspot. The instrument does not resist you or punish you for exploring. It meets you where you are.

This was by design, and it was by love.

Moth takes its name and its nature from one of the oldest metaphors in Hindu and Vedic philosophy: the moth drawn to flame. In the Bhagavad Gita and across centuries of Indian spiritual thought, the moth is not a cautionary symbol — not foolishness flying toward destruction. It is the soul in its most faithful expression: moving without reservation toward the light, giving itself completely, holding nothing back. The Sanskrit tradition understands the soul — atman — as indestructible, continuous, and in eternal relationship with the divine. The moth does not die in the flame. It becomes it.

The person for whom this instrument is named carried that quality in his living. He gave love unquestioningly, without condition or calculation, and in doing so drew love back to himself as naturally as the moth draws toward the glow. He transformed quietly. He moved toward warmth. He left everyone around him a little more themselves.

His name is woven into the root of every instance of Moth that will ever exist. Not as a dedication on a wall, but genetically — constitutively. The unique character that makes your instrument yours traces its lineage back, in part, to him.

There is a Hindu concept that sits at the heart of all of this: that those we love do not simply leave when they go. They are not an absence to be grieved, but an inheritance to be carried. The qualities we cherished in them become ours — not by taking, but by continuing. We do not mourn them forward. We bring them forward.

Every person who plays a Moth and finds it warm, finds it responsive, finds it somehow generous — is touching something real. Something that was given freely, and passes freely on.

That is why yours is unique.
That is why it is alive.
That is why it is called Moth.

Each instance of this instrument is generated once and never regenerated. Your Moth is yours alone.
